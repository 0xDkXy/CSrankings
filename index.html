Computer Science Rankings (beta)

This ranking is designed to be entirely objective — unlike US News and World Report's, which is exclusively based on surveys — and difficult to game. Unlike other rankings, the only way to rise here is to publish more at the most selective venues, which is what most academics already aim to do. Contrast this with citation-based metrics, which have been repeatedly shown to be easy to manipulate.

The areas and the conferences listed (at most three per area) were developed in consultation with faculty across a range of institutions. They are meant to span most established research-centric subareas of computer science, but are not necessarily comprehensive. The rubrics (AI, Programming Languages, Systems, and Theory) are provided exclusively for comparison with rankings published by US News and World Report.

Output is attributed to the institution where authors are currently affiliated full-time, tenure-track faculty members. Note that this means an author's publications follow them if they move. Currently, this database includes only U.S. CS or EECS departments (or equivalent).

Adjusted counts: each publication is counted exactly once, with credit adjusted by splitting evenly across all authors. This approach makes it impossible to boost rankings simply by adding authors to a paper. Average percent computes the mean percentage of (adjusted) publications in each area by institution. This weighs areas with different publication rates evenly. Total count computes the adjusted sum of all papers published, which effectively emphasizes areas with more publications.

Click on a triangle (►) to display names and number of publications. Click on a name to go to a faculty member's home page; click on the raw number of publications to go to their DBLP entry. Hover over the adjusted number (divided among all co-authors) to list senior co-authors.
